How to Choose a Licensed Contractor in Hawaii

Fix-It Hawaii Team · January 2026

Hiring the wrong contractor in Hawaii can cost you thousands in substandard work, unpermitted construction that must be redone, or worse — liability for injuries on your property by an uninsured worker. Hawaii has specific licensing requirements for contractors, and protecting yourself begins with understanding the system.

Hawaii's Contractor Licensing System

In Hawaii, contractors must be licensed by the Department of Commerce and Consumer Affairs (DCCA) Contractors License Board. There are two primary license types relevant to homeowners: the General Engineering Contractor (Class A), the General Building Contractor (Class B), and Specialty Contractors (Class C) which cover specific trades like electrical, plumbing, painting, and others. A handyman who performs a single trade is typically licensed as a Class C specialty contractor. Always verify — you can check license status at pvl.ehawaii.gov.

Why Unlicensed Work Is Risky

Beyond quality concerns, unlicensed contractor work carries serious legal and financial risks. If an unlicensed worker is injured on your property, your homeowner's insurance may not cover the claim — leaving you personally liable. Unpermitted construction (which unlicensed contractors often perform to avoid scrutiny) creates title encumbrances that must be resolved — often expensively — when you sell. Insurance claims related to improper unlicensed work are routinely denied.

Verify Insurance Before Signing Anything

Ask for a Certificate of Insurance showing general liability coverage (minimum $300,000; $1M+ preferred for larger projects) and workers' compensation if the contractor has employees. Call the insurance company directly to verify the policy is active — certificates can be forged or reflect canceled policies. Fix-It Hawaii carries full liability insurance and is happy to provide certificate copies upon request.

Get Everything in Writing

A verbal agreement is not a contract. Every job should have a written agreement covering: exact scope of work, specific materials to be used (brand, model, grade), total price and payment schedule, start and estimated completion dates, warranty terms, and process for handling change orders. Never pay more than 10–33% upfront — Hawaii's contractor licensing law limits the down payment a licensed contractor can legally require.

Check References and Portfolio

Ask for 3 references from similar projects completed within the past 2 years — and actually call them. Ask not just whether they were satisfied, but whether the project came in on budget, whether the contractor showed up as scheduled, and whether they'd hire them again. Photos in a portfolio are meaningful only if you can verify they're actually the contractor's work.

Red Flags to Avoid

Be cautious of: contractors who can only be reached through a cell phone with no business address, pressure to pay fully in cash before work begins, inability to produce a license number, "too good to be true" quotes significantly below other estimates, and contractors who suggest skipping permits to "save you money." None of these situations save you money — they transfer risk to you.
