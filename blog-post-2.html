Hurricane Prep: How to Protect Your Kauai Home

Fix-It Hawaii Team · May 2026

Kauai residents know firsthand that hurricane threats are real — Hurricane Iniki devastated the island in 1992, and the increasing frequency of tropical storms in the Central Pacific means hurricane preparedness is not optional for Kauai homeowners. Here's a practical, room-by-room guide to protecting your home before a storm hits.

Start Before Hurricane Season (Before June 1)

The worst time to prepare is when a hurricane is approaching. Before the season begins, inspect and reinforce your roof. Have a professional check flashing, fascia, and ridge cap fasteners. Poorly secured roofs are the leading cause of catastrophic hurricane damage. Trim all trees within 30 feet of your home — a dead limb in 100mph winds becomes a projectile. Document your home's interior with photos or video for insurance purposes and store a copy off-island or in the cloud.

Windows and Doors

Windows and doors are your home's most vulnerable points. Install hurricane shutters on all exterior windows — aluminum roll-down shutters are most convenient, but plywood panels provide adequate protection in a pinch. For sliding glass doors, use a security bar in the track AND verify the door frame is properly anchored — sliding door failure is extremely common in hurricanes.

Garage Doors

Garage doors are the largest and most vulnerable opening in most homes. Standard residential garage doors are not rated for hurricane winds. If your home has a garage, install a wind-load rated hurricane door or brace the existing door with a manufacturer-approved bracing kit. An unbraced garage door failing in a hurricane commonly leads to complete roof loss.

Outdoor Areas and Landscaping

Bring all patio furniture, potted plants, grills, and decorations inside. Items you can't move should be strapped down or removed. Pool furniture, trampolines, and inflatable items must be secured or stored — these become dangerous projectiles in high winds. Stock outdoor storage sheds with a padlock, as even lightweight items can cause serious damage.

Emergency Supply Preparation

FEMA recommends a minimum 72-hour supply kit. For Kauai, we recommend at least 7–14 days given the island's supply chain vulnerabilities after a major storm. Essentials include: drinking water (1 gallon per person per day), non-perishable food, first aid kit, flashlights and batteries, battery-powered or hand-crank radio, medications, cash, and important documents in waterproof storage.

Post-Storm Safety

After a hurricane passes, do not enter your home until emergency services declare it safe. Be aware of downed power lines, structural damage, and gas leaks. Document all damage thoroughly before making any repairs — your insurance claim depends on thorough documentation. Preparation is the best protection. Invest the time and modest cost of preparation now — the alternative is far more expensive and stressful when a storm is already in the forecast.
