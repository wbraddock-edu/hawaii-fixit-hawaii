Common Repairs in Older Hawaii Homes (And What They Cost)

Fix-It Hawaii Team · April 2026

Hawaii's housing stock includes many homes built between the 1950s and 1980s — an era when construction standards were different, materials have now aged significantly, and island-specific challenges have had decades to accumulate. If you own or are buying an older Hawaii home, understanding the most common issues — and their realistic repair costs — is essential.

Termite and Pest Damage

Hawaii has both subterranean and drywood termite species, and older homes — particularly those with original wood framing and minimal chemical treatment history — often have significant termite damage. Signs include hollow-sounding wood, small exit holes in wood surfaces, and frass (termite droppings). Repair costs range widely: small localized damage can be $500–$2,000; major structural framing damage can reach $15,000–$50,000+. Annual inspection by a licensed pest control company is non-negotiable for older Hawaii properties.

Outdated Electrical Systems

Homes built before 1975 may have aluminum wiring (a fire risk) or Federal Pacific and Zinsco electrical panels, both of which have documented failure rates and are rejected by most insurance companies. Replacing an outdated panel runs $2,000–$4,500. Aluminum wiring remediation (adding COPALUM connectors to all connections) typically costs $3,000–$6,000 for a standard home.

Cast Iron and Galvanized Plumbing

Galvanized steel supply pipes from the pre-1970 era corrode internally over time, restricting water flow and eventually failing. Cast iron drain lines suffer similar internal corrosion. A full plumbing repipe of a 3-bedroom home in Hawaii costs $8,000–$15,000. Spot repairs of isolated sections are possible at $500–$2,500 per section, though whole-house repipes are more economical long-term.

Roof Systems

Three-tab asphalt shingles installed in the 1980s and 1990s are likely at or past their expected lifespan. In Hawaii's UV environment, shingles degrade faster than on the mainland. A new roof on a 1,500 sq ft home runs $12,000–$22,000 depending on material. Metal roofing — more expensive upfront at $18,000–$30,000 — offers dramatically longer lifespan and better hurricane performance.

Lanai and Deck Deterioration

Wooden lanais and decks in Hawaii face relentless humidity, UV exposure, and often inadequate original waterproofing. Rot in posts, beams, and decking boards is common. A full lanai rebuild costs $8,000–$25,000. Partial repairs focusing on rot removal and board replacement can extend serviceable life for $1,500–$5,000 with proper treatment.

Attic Ventilation and Moisture Issues

Many older Hawaii homes have inadequate attic ventilation, leading to moisture buildup that causes mold growth and accelerates wood deterioration. Adding ridge vents, soffit vents, or powered attic fans costs $500–$2,000 and can dramatically improve attic conditions and HVAC efficiency. Mold remediation — if moisture problems have gone unaddressed — can cost $2,000–$10,000+.
